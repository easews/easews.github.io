Admissibility of Evidence

The lesson plan considers the admissibility of evidence and the significant role the judge plays as gatekeeper and the role of investigators in supporting them in that role. The activity plan supports individuals in developing understanding and awareness around handling evidence and consideration of expert evidence.

Digital forensic techniques are also applied in a much broader range of inquiries, such as internal corporate investigations, that often do not result in formal proceedings in public court. Despite the fact that investigations may not require the same standard of proof, forensic analysts should always follow sound forensic practices in collecting and analysing the artifacts. This includes adherence to any judicial requirements when working with inherently personal data, which can be a non-trivial concern when the investigation is multi-jurisdictional. In such cases, it is important to seek timely legal advice to preserve the integrity of the inquiry. CyBOK Forensic Knowledge Area. Pg 5.

Aims, outcomes and opportunities

The aim, intended learning outcomes and skills of the session are highlighted and discussed with learners. The aim of this session is for students to consider the significance and importance of handling evidence and the consideration of expert evidence.

Intended Learning Outcomes

The expectation is that by the end of this activity, individuals will be able to:

  1. understand the cross jurisdictional nature of investigations involving cyber systems.

  2. admissibility of evidence in the context of a court room and how that can be undermined by actions performed during an investigation.

  3. appreciation of the challenge of admitting expert evidence and the different approaches.

Skills development

The expectation is that this activity will afford the following skills development opportunities:

  • investigation skills in terms of pursuing new knowledge and information as they identify existing cases.

  • collaboration in working with others to understand their perspectives and views in developing deeper insight into concepts.

  • communication skills in terms of communicating discovered case study and relevant points.

Friend Flummox

An engaging activity to motivate learners to engage with the admissibility of evidence material. Learners are required to devise an exam question that will challenge a peer in the class on the topic of admissible evidence.

The goal of the activity is to focus individuals and to generate curiosity as well as to provide an opportunity for learners to develop interpersonal and communication skills.

Instructions

The lecturer or instructor should:

  1. Advise class that they are required to create an exam question that probes understanding of admissible evidence but also challenges their peers. Learners can be as creative as they want in design of the question, for example they do not need to restrict themselves to text.

  2. Give the learners around ten minutes to create the question but will want to ensure energy in the room by walking through the class and advise when time is running out.

  3. Then instruct learners to form pairs or trios and exchange their exam questions. The pairs or trios then have 10 minutes to tackle the question. The questions do not need to be in exam conditions, learners can have fun and help each other out.

  4. Collect all the exam questions from the learners.

Themes from Friend Flummox Activity

The aim of the prior activity is to drive curiosity and interest in the challenge of admissible evidence. The lecturer should now focus the class by considering the themes of that emerged in the exam questions and steer them to areas of focus.

Instructions

The lecturer or instructor should:

  1. Ask learners to nominate any particular good exam questions. Nominators should present the exam question to the class and state the advantages of the approach.

  2. Open the question to the class and ask them to comment on the question and how they would approach it.

  3. Repeat process until a number of questions have been considered. Open an audience response system activity, such as Mentimeter, ask students to submit some of the themes that emerge from these questions in terms of areas of focus.

  4. Steer the consideration of themes along the significant of the judge as gatekeeper and how digital investigators can support the judge in that role.

Considering the Expert Evidence from Detective Mark Lounsbury

Pairs of learners are to consider the case and expert testimony to consider if there are any ground for appeal. The activity acts as the first step in learners considering what it is to be an expert and when expert evidence should be admitted and on what grounds.

Instructions

The lecturer or instructor should:

  1. Issue the activity sheet that will guide and support the learners.

  2. Advise learners they have to self-organise into pairs and are going to consider the State of Connecticut v. Julie Amero case and court transcript from Detective Mark Lounsbury.

  3. Pairs are to determine whether there could be grounds for an appeal based on the quality and accuracy of the expert testimony.

  4. Permit pairs specific time to consider their summary and transcript snippets to determine if there are grounds for appeal.

  5. Permit pairs specific time to produce a 100 word judgement and this will support them in subsquent activities. Advise pairs they should consider specific aspects when reaching their judgement, specifically: establishing intent to access website accuracy of expert testimony and alternative explanations or positions.

  6. As pairs consider and document their judgement, the lectuer should wander between them and address any questions or gaps in understanding.

Materials

  • Activity Sheet
  • State of Connecticut v. Julie Amero case
  • 250-word summary (produced in advance by learner).
  • Detective Mark Lounsbury Transcript.

Generating guidelines for courts to admit expert evidence

Pairs should form start-up firms of no more than FOUR members and exchange summaries, summary positions adopted from consideration of prior expert testimony and behaviour of members of the court. The firms are expected to produce guidelines that would support judges in admitting expert evidence to court. Firms will then present their guidelines to the class for further discussion.

Instructions

The lecturer or instructor should:

  1. Issue the activity sheet that will guide and support the learners.

  2. Advise learners they are going to consider the State of Connecticut v. Julie Amero case case, overall handling of evidence and that they are going to generate no more than FOUR guidelines that support judges in admitting expert evidence.

  3. Advise pairs to self-organise into teams, exchange summaries and judgements from previous activities.

  4. Permit teams time to discuss and consider their summaries, judgements from previous activities.

  5. Instruct teams to generate ONE presentation slide that succinctly outlines each of the guidelines as well as brief rationale for each.

  6. As teams consider and document their judgement, the lectuer should wander between them and address any questions or gaps in understanding.

  7. Advise teams to upload slide to the virtual learning environment (VLE) when time has elasped.

Materials

  • Activity Sheet
  • State of Connecticut v. Julie Amero case.
  • 250-word summary (produced in advance by learner).
  • Summary position adopted for Detective Mark Lounsbury (outcome of prior activity).
  • Summary position adopted for Bob Hartz (outcome of prior activity).
  • Summary position adopted for Court Members (outcome of prior activity).

Discussing Emering Themes in Guidelines

Teams will present the themes they have identified from a previous activity as well as engage in a discussion with the rest of the class.

Instructions

The lecturer or instructor should:

  1. The lecturer will use a random number generator to randomly select pairs to present. The lecturer may favour to select pairs on what they have observed, rather than randomly to demonstrate and discuss with the entire class any novel insights or gaps in understanding.

  2. Teams have two minutes to present the themes they have identified as well as divergence.

  3. The lecturer should select one of the themes or divergence to discuss with the wider class and ask for their comments and thoughts.

  4. The lecturer should continue to ask for teams to present either based on the disucssion, what they observed when teams were producing their slides or using a random number generator.
