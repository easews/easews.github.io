Sequencing Events and Information Processing Active Lesson Plan

Overview

The lesson plan considers conceptual models from the perspective of the steps, processes and loops that investigators consider during the information processing process as part of digital investigations.

The lesson symmetrical progresses through the cognitive task model presented in the CyBOK. The first layer is to consider the key steps, the second layer considers the abstract and analytical subprocesses and finally the third layer considers the loops to extract and/or enhance data. The approach is sat within the context of the information crisis facing digital forensics.

The class progresses through the consideration of the cognitive task model by devising their own models, processes and loops formed through consideration of case studies they have identified and selected. An approach that affords the development of a number of skills and attainment of aims as well as intended learning outcomes.

Aims, outcomes and opportunities

The aim, intended learning outcomes and skills of the session are highlighted and discussed with students. The aim of this session is for students to consider the significance and importance of digital investigation processes and procedures in conducting effective investigations.

Aims

The focus of the session of the significance of digital investigation models in the context of the crisis of information.

Intended Learning Outcomes

The intended learning outcomes for the session are that students at the end of the lesson will be able to:

  1. understand the major steps in information processing and how to expand and reduce collection of information in response to a particular scenarios.

  2. understand the processes involved in understanding the sequence of events for a particular incident.

  3. awareness of the iterative loops that individuals have to perform within investigation to improve insight and enhance data.

  4. appreciation of the overall theory of the cognitive task model and how it can used to identify pressure points for investigations.

Skills development

The expectation is that this activity will afford the following skills development opportunities:

  1. investigation skills in terms of pursuing new knowledge and information as they identify existing cases.

  2. collaboration in working with others to understand their perspectives and views in developing deeper insight into concepts.

  3. specialist knowledge in terms of the major steps and elements of the digital investigation procedures.

  4. communication skills in terms of communicating discovered case study and relevant points.

Devising the Cognitive Steps for Information Processing in Digital Investigations

The aim is for pairs to formulate the key steps involved in the digital investigation procedure rather than simply read about them.

Learners are expected to identify the major steps in information processing, starting with data sources and progressing to presentation of the case through development of a mind map.

Instructions

The lecturer or instructor should:

  1. Lecturer should issue the activity sheet and advise pairs to consider the major steps in information processing of digital investigation in the context of their selected case study.

  2. Lecture must advise pairs that they have to produce a presentation and that some pairs will be randomly selected to present. If the class is small enough then all pairs can present. However, in the situation where the class is large, ensuring learners are aware that they may have to present will ensure they are sufficiently motivated to complete the activity.

  3. Lecturer should advise pairs to consider the incomplete cognitive model and start to think about what intervening actions an investigator would take to present a case from a selection of initial data sources.

  4. Pairs should then consider these different elements to distill them down to central steps in the information processing of a case from the perspective of digital investigations.

  5. Lecturer should wander between pairs and consider the major steps that groups are identifying so that they can be called upon later during a class-wide presentations.

  6. Lastly, pairs should structure the task model of the major steps that support progression between steps.

  7. Lecture should collect presentations and task models from students.

Materials

  • Activity Sheet
  • Incomplete Cognitive Model

Class collective development of Cognitive Steps

The aim is for students to collectively agree the key steps in the task of information processing within the digital investigation. In many the class is generating the cognitive task model or a variant of it that is discussed in the CyBOK prior to consideration of it.

Instructions

The lecturer or instructor should:

  1. The lecturer will use a random number generator to randomly select pairs to present. The lecturer may favour to select pairs on what they have observed, rather than randomly to demostrate and discuss with the entire class any novel insights or gaps in understanding.

  2. The lecturer should present the starting and ending point for most digital investigation in terms of information processing, i.e. starting from a number of data sources to the presentation of a case.

  3. The lecturer will then ask class to offer the next step, the lecturer will then take answers and discuss them with the class. Asking for further possible steps before starting to settle on a specific step. Lecturer will also want to shape what groups the call on based on their experience of wander the class and collected task models.

  4. Before moving onto to consider the next step in the process at each turn the lecturer should seek confirmation from the class. Lecturers may want to consider an audience response system, such Mentimeter.

  5. The lecturer should then conclude the session with a complete, class generated task model from all the case studies considered.

Refining task model with analytical loops

Learners are expected to revisit the class wide task model generated previously with processes and consider the high-frequency iterative loops employed by investigators to strengthen their case.

Learners are to consider one bottom-up transformational loop and one top-down transformation loop from the perspective of strengthening the case.

Instructions

The lecturer or instructor should:

  1. Lecturer should issue the incomplete class-wide task model with key steps and processes.

  2. The lecturer should advise learners to consider the material on analytical loops that investigators would employ to strengthen their case.

  3. The lecturer should initially ask learners to consider the case and identify a point where the investigator may have developed an initial theory but had no supporting data.

  4. The lecturer should then ask groups when is that likely to occur within the information processing process in the digital investigation and what actions did the investigator perform.

  5. Pairs should then annotate the task model with the relevant analytical loop.

  6. Pairs should then consider a the case and consider a point where the investigator encountered fragments of evidence but was unsure what connected them.

  7. Pairs should then consider when that is likely to occur within the information processing process in the digital investigation and what actions did the investigator perform.

  8. Pairs should then annotate the task model with the relevant analytical loop.

  9. The lecturer should advise pairs to take a snapshot of the annonted model and upload it to the virtual learning environment (VLE).

Class collective development of Analytical Loops

The aim is for the class to identify the two prominent analytical loops to strengthen cases. The expectation is that the class will identify many loops but the lecturer can shape and discuss these into the loops observed in the cognitive task model presented in the CyBOK and discussed in the debriefing session that follows the activity.

Instructions

The lecturer or instructor should:

  1. The lecturer will use a random number generator to randomly select pairs to present. The lecturer may favour to select a pair on what they have observed, rather than randomly to demostrate and discuss with the entire class any novel insights or gaps in understanding.

  2. Lecturer should then open-up to class and suggest what the investigator would do in such a situation. Lecturer should take one example and note down, offer one if none provided.

  3. Lecturer should ask does any other pair if they have a different suggestion or approach.

  4. Lecturer should then ask the pair that presented the example, what the investigator actually did or what the group felt they likely did, if it not clear.

  5. Lecturer should then discuss at what point such activity is likely to happen within the information processing process with the class and achieve consensus. Lecturer may consider using audience participation tools, such as Mentimeter.

  6. Lecturer should move on to consider the loop where an investigator may have data but is not clear how it relates or what it means. The lecturer can again use a random number generator to randomly select pairs to present or select a pair on what they have observed previously.

  7. The lecturer should then ask the class what they think the investigator would do in such a situation.

  8. Lecturer should then ask if there are any other suggestions from others, before asking the presenting pair to state their perception.

  9. Lecturer should then discuss at what point such activity is likely to happen within the information processing process with the class and achieve consensus. Lecturer may consider using audience participation tools, such as Mentimeter.
